All the streets of Babylon, leading on each side to the river, were secured by two-leaved brazen gates, and these were providentially left open when Cyrus’s forces entered the city in the night, through the channel of the river, in the general disorder occasioned by the great feast which was then celebrated; otherwise, says Herodotus (i. 180. 191), the Persians would have been shut up in the bed of the river, as in a net, and all destroyed. The gates of the palace were also imprudently opened to ascertain the occasion of the tumult; when the two parties under Gobrias and Gadatas rushed in, got possession of the palace, and slew the king (Xenophon, lib. vii).
